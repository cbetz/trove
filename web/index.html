trove

A comparison tool for hospital charity care reporting.

Every nonprofit U.S. hospital files Worksheet S-10 with CMS and Schedule H with the IRS. Both forms ask, in different language, for the same number: the cost of charity care provided to patients who couldn't pay. The forms aren't required to match, and they often don't. trove makes the comparison computable across hospital systems for tax year 2022.

This is a side-by-side reconciliation tool, not an accusation engine. A nonzero gap is a starting point for review, not evidence of misconduct — many divergences are explained by definitional differences (Medicaid shortfall handling, partial-payment netting), timing (fiscal years that don't match calendar tax years), or the legitimate fact that some hospitals don't bill Medicare in volume. The interesting cases are the ones that aren't explained by those.

Each row is one nonprofit hospital system — one IRS Employer Identification Number (EIN) — aggregating the underlying CMS Worksheet S-10 numbers across every CCN-keyed facility that files under that EIN. The 990 figure comes from Schedule H Part I line 7a (financial assistance at cost). The HCRIS figure is Worksheet S-10 charity care cost.

Default view: well-aligned, comparable systems only. HCRIS uses a federal-fiscal-year reporting cycle, not a fiscal period; for many hospitals, CMS reporting cycle "FY2023" actually contains a report covering a 12-month period that doesn't overlap with their TY2022 990 at all. The default filter restricts to systems where the two filings cover the same period (within 1 month) and both report at least $500K — the only subset where the comparison is genuinely apples-to-apples. Toggle the filters off if you want the unfiltered view.

How to read the leaderboard.

The default view filters to aligned, comparable systems: HCRIS fiscal year end ≈ 990 tax period end (within 1 month) and both filings report ≥ $500K of charity care. The default sort ranks those by absolute proportional gap — the hospitals where both regulators heard a real number but the numbers disagree most. Click any row to see the underlying numbers, period alignment, and a link to the actual 990 filing on ProPublica.

A row appearing high here means: (a) we matched the system across HCRIS and 990 correctly, (b) the periods cover the same year, and (c) the two filings still disagree. It does not mean the hospital is hiding charity care — see the methodology below for the dozen reasons two filings can legitimately diverge.

Method, definitions, and what this doesn't show

Why HCRIS S-10 charity care cost ≠ Schedule H Part I 7a, even when both are reported correctly
  • Definitional differences. Schedule H 7a excludes some categories that HCRIS S-10 may include — Medicaid shortfall handling, partial-payment netting, charity-vs.-uninsured-discount classification.
  • Cost-to-charge ratio (CCR) handling. HCRIS S-10 applies a hospital-wide CCR; Schedule H allows different cost-step-down approaches.
  • Charity care policy boundaries. Hospitals can categorize the same patient case as charity care, bad debt, or contractual allowance — moving cost between lines.
  • Multi-facility aggregation. A single 990 EIN often covers several HCRIS-reporting facilities. Summing facility-level charity care up to the system level can smooth or amplify reporting differences across facilities.
  • Non-Medicare hospitals. Children's hospitals, specialty cancer centers, and some teaching hospitals don't bill Medicare in volume, so HCRIS S-10 charity care can legitimately be near zero while Schedule H reports millions.
The HCRIS-vs-990 fiscal year alignment problem

HCRIS uses the federal fiscal year as a reporting cycle, not as a fiscal period. The file labeled "FY2023" contains hospital reports submitted during federal fiscal year 2023 — but the periods those reports cover vary by hospital fiscal calendar:

  • For July–June fiscal-year hospitals, "FY2023" mostly contains their report for the year that ended June 2024.
  • For calendar-year hospitals, "FY2023" mostly contains their CY2023 report (ending Dec 2023).
  • For October–September hospitals, "FY2023" mostly contains their report ending September 2023.

None of those periods perfectly aligns with 990 Tax Year 2022 (which itself varies by filer fiscal calendar). The default filter restricts to systems where the two filings actually cover the same period — about 17% of the 1,334 matched systems. The rest are visible if you turn the alignment filter off, but those rows are comparing different years and shouldn't be trusted as direct comparisons.

Coming in v2: per-hospital matching across HCRIS reporting cycles 2022/2023/2024 to find the report whose period actually matches each hospital's TY2022 990. This will substantially expand the well-aligned set.

Coverage and known limits
  • Crosswalk: 3,079 of 6,042 HCRIS hospitals appear in CBI's December 2024 crosswalk; the unmatched ~50% are mostly for-profit and government hospitals (no 990) plus hospitals not in CBI's frozen vintage. Of 1,481 unique TY2022 990 EINs ingested, 1,334 match the crosswalk.
  • Time window: HCRIS Fiscal Year 2023 paired with 990 Tax Year 2022, ingested across IRS release years 2024, 2025, and 2026. TY2022 amendments filed after the 2026 quarterly cutoff aren't included.
  • Validation: 6 spot-checked EINs (Memorial Hermann, St Jude, Yale, Western Regional, UVA Prince William, CHI St Vincent) match the IRS source XML exact-to-the-dollar. Parser is verified clean. Not a guarantee that every row is parsed correctly — bug reports welcome at the GitHub issues link.
  • Not a leaderboard of suspicion. The data is a starting point for review, not a definitive accusation. The value is making the comparison computable at scale, where previously every researcher had to reconstruct it from raw filings.
